📐 Dimensionality Reduction – PCA

Turn many columns into a few “summary” directions. Principal Component Analysis explained like you're 5!

Use these with this lesson: wine_data.csv  |  Dimension_Reduction_PCA.pdf (slides)  |  Session notebook: pca.ipynb (in Downloads → Dimensionality Reduction - PCA). Save the CSV in the same folder as your notebook so pd.read_csv("wine_data.csv") works.

Part 1: What is Dimensionality Reduction?

Dimensionality = how many columns (features) your data has. Dimensionality reduction = making fewer columns while keeping as much useful information as possible.

👶 In One Sentence (Like You're 5)

PCA (Principal Component Analysis) finds a small set of new “summary” directions (called principal components) that capture most of the variation in your data—so you can reduce 100 columns to a few for visualization or faster modeling, with minimal information loss.

📺 The TV Summary Analogy

Imagine a 3-hour movie. You can’t show the whole thing in 30 seconds—but you can show the trailer: the most important scenes that give you the gist. PCA does the same for your data: it finds the “trailer” directions that summarize most of what’s going on, so you need fewer numbers to understand the story.

Why “Principal Components”?

Principal = main, most important. Component = one direction or axis. So PCA finds the “main directions” in your data—the directions along which the data varies the most. The first principal component is the single direction with the most spread; the second is the next best (and perpendicular to the first), and so on.

Part 2: Why Do We Need PCA?

When you have too many columns, three big problems show up. PCA helps with all of them.

🔀 The “Overlapping” Problem PCA Fixes

Many columns often say the same thing in different ways. For example: “height in cm,” “height in inches,” and “tall / not tall” all describe one idea. That’s overlapping information—like telling the same story three times. PCA finds the main “summary” directions and merges that overlap into a few uncorrelated components, so you don’t count the same signal twice and your data is easier to visualize and model.

Problem How PCA Helps
Visualization Humans can only easily see 2D or 3D. If you have 50 columns, you can’t plot them all. PCA lets you squash 50 columns into 2 or 3 “summary” columns and plot those.
Speed & overfitting More columns = slower models and higher risk of overfitting. Fewer columns (after PCA) = faster training and often better generalization.
Noise Some columns are mostly noise or repeat what others say. PCA keeps the directions with the most signal and drops the rest.

🍎 Layman Example 1: The Apple

You describe an apple by 100 measurements: weight, color in 10 wavelengths, 20 shape stats, 30 texture numbers, etc. Most of that is redundant or noise. PCA might find that “size + redness + roundness” (3 numbers) capture 95% of what makes apples different. Now you can work with 3 numbers instead of 100.

📷 Layman Example 2: Photo Compression

A photo has millions of pixels—way too many to “see” at once. Compression (like JPEG) finds a few patterns that summarize the image: “mostly blue here,” “edge there.” PCA does the same for tables: it finds a few “patterns” (principal components) that summarize your columns, so you keep the gist without keeping every number.

🍳 Layman Example 3: Recipe in 3 Lines

A recipe has 50 ingredients and 20 steps. You can’t tell a friend all of that in one breath. So you say: “It’s sweet, spicy, and creamy.” Those three “summary directions” capture most of what the dish is. PCA finds the data version of “sweet, spicy, creamy” for your columns—a few summary directions that capture most of what’s going on.

Part 3: How Does PCA Work? (Simple Idea)

You don’t need the math to get the idea. Here’s the intuition.

Steps (in plain English)

1

Center the data – Subtract the mean from each column so the data is centered around zero.

2

Find the direction of maximum spread – PCA finds the line (in 2D) or hyperplane direction along which the data varies the most. That’s the first principal component (PC1).

3

Find the next best direction – The second principal component (PC2) is the direction with the next most variation, and it’s perpendicular to PC1. Same for PC3, PC4, …

4

Project your data – Each row is then “projected” onto these new directions. You get new columns: PC1, PC2, … If you keep only PC1 and PC2, you’ve reduced many columns to 2.

🖼️ Shadow Analogy

Think of your data as a cloud of points in 3D. You shine a light from one direction and look at the shadow on a wall. PCA chooses the light direction so that the shadow is as “spread out” as possible—that shadow is like your first principal component. Then it finds the next light direction (perpendicular to the first) that spreads the data the most, and so on.

Idea: Many columns → a few “summary” directions

Variance Explained

After fitting PCA, you can see how much “variance” each component captures. For example: PC1 = 60%, PC2 = 25%, PC3 = 10% … So with 2 components you might explain 85% of the variation—often that’s enough for visualization or a simpler model.

Part 4: When to Use PCA

  • Visualization – You have many features and want a 2D or 3D plot (e.g. after clustering).
  • Too many features – Models are slow or overfit; reducing dimensions can help.
  • Multicollinearity – Many columns are highly correlated; PCA gives you uncorrelated components.
  • Noise – You believe many dimensions are noise; PCA keeps high-variance (signal) directions.

When NOT to use PCA

Don’t use PCA if you need to interpret original features (e.g. “which gene matters?”). PCA components are mixes of all columns and don’t map back to single features. For interpretation, use feature selection or other methods. Also, always scale your data (e.g. StandardScaler) before PCA, since it’s sensitive to units.

Part 5: PCA in Python (sklearn)

Use sklearn.decomposition.PCA. Steps: load data → scale → fit PCA → transform (or fit_transform).

import pandas as pd
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA

# 1) Load your data (example: many numeric columns)
df = pd.read_csv('your_data.csv')
X = df.select_dtypes(include=['number']).dropna(axis=1)

# 2) Scale features (important for PCA!)
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X)

# 3) Fit PCA (e.g. keep 2 components for plotting)
pca = PCA(n_components=2)
X_pca = pca.fit_transform(X_scaled)

# 4) See how much variance each component explains
print("Variance explained:", pca.explained_variance_ratio_)
print("Total (2 components):", pca.explained_variance_ratio_.sum())

Now X_pca has 2 columns (PC1, PC2). You can plot them, feed them to clustering, or use more components for modeling.

How many components?

  • For visualization: use n_components=2 or 3.
  • For “keep 95% variance”: use PCA(n_components=0.95) and sklearn picks the number of components that explain 95% of the variance.
# Optional: keep enough components to explain 95% of variance
pca95 = PCA(n_components=0.95)
X_pca95 = pca95.fit_transform(X_scaled)
print("Number of components:", pca95.n_components_)

Part 6: Full Example – Wine Data (Line by Line)

We use the Wine dataset: 13 chemical features (Alcohol, Malic_Acid, Ash, …) and a Customer_Segment (1, 2, or 3). The goal is to visualize the data in 2D by reducing 13 columns to 2 principal components and coloring points by segment. Same philosophy as the rest of the site: every step explained like you're 5.

PROBLEM

Visualize the data in a 2D plot based on customer segment. We can't plot 13 axes—so we use PCA to get 2 “summary” directions (PC1, PC2) and plot those. Customer_Segment is only for coloring; we are not predicting it.

Step 1: Load and understand the data

data = pd.read_csv("wine_data.csv")
data.head(10)
data["Customer_Segment"].value_counts()

What this does

  • read_csv("wine_data.csv") loads the table. Put wine_data.csv in the same folder as your notebook.
  • head(10) shows the first 10 rows: 13 numeric columns (Alcohol, Malic_Acid, …) plus Customer_Segment (1, 2, or 3).
  • value_counts() shows how many rows per segment (e.g. 2→71, 1→59, 3→48). We use this only for coloring the 2D plot later.

Step 2: Train–test split and scale

We split data and scale features so no single column (e.g. Proline) dominates just because of its units. PCA is sensitive to scale!

def train_test_split_and_scale(data):
    y = data["Customer_Segment"]   # only for coloring, not for prediction
    x = data.drop('Customer_Segment', axis=1)
    x_train, x_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(x, y, test_size=0.20, random_state=0)
    scaler = StandardScaler()
    x_train = scaler.fit_transform(x_train)
    x_test = scaler.transform(x_test)
    return x_train, x_test, y_train, y_test

x_train, x_test, y_train, y_test, features = train_test_split_and_scale(data)

👶 Why scale?

If one column is in thousands (e.g. Proline) and another in single digits, PCA would think the big column “matters more.” Scaling puts everyone on the same playing field so we find directions of real variation, not just units.

Step 3: Apply PCA and see variance explained

from sklearn.decomposition import PCA

pca = PCA(n_components=5)
x_train_pca = pca.fit_transform(x_train)
x_test_pca = pca.transform(x_test)

print("Explained Variance Ratio:", pca.explained_variance_ratio_)

You might see something like: [0.37, 0.19, 0.11, 0.07, 0.06]. So the first component captures 37% of the variance, the second 19%, etc. The first two together capture about 56%—enough for a rough 2D picture.

Step 4: How many components? (Variance curve)

To choose how many components to keep, we often plot number of components vs total variance explained. For modeling, many people keep enough components to explain at least 90% variance.

explained_variance = []
components = []
for z in range(2, 14):
    pca = PCA(n_components=z)
    x_train_pca = pca.fit_transform(x_train)
    exp_variance = pca.explained_variance_ratio_.sum()
    components.append(z)
    explained_variance.append(exp_variance)
    print("Components:", z, "| Variance explained:", round(exp_variance, 4))

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
plt.plot(components, explained_variance, 'bx-')
plt.xlabel('Number of PCA components')
plt.ylabel('Explained Variance Ratio')
plt.title('PCA and Explained Variance')
plt.show()

Rule of thumb

For visualization, use 2 (or 3) components. For building models on top of PCA, choose the number of components that explain at least 90% of the variance (e.g. 8–9 in this dataset).

Step 5: Visualize in 2D (PC1 vs PC2, colored by segment)

pca = PCA(n_components=2)
x_train_pca = pca.fit_transform(x_train)

y_train = y_train.reset_index(drop=True)
pca_dataframe = pd.DataFrame(x_train_pca)
pca_dataframe['Customer_Segment'] = y_train
pca_dataframe.columns = ['Principal Component 1', 'Principal Component 2', 'Customer_Segment']

import seaborn as sns
sns.scatterplot(data=pca_dataframe, x='Principal Component 1', y='Principal Component 2', hue='Customer_Segment', palette="deep")
plt.title('PCA with 2 components')
plt.xlabel('Principal Component 1')
plt.ylabel('Principal Component 2')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

What you get

A 2D scatter plot: each point is a wine sample. The x-axis is PC1, the y-axis is PC2. Colors = Customer_Segment (1, 2, 3). You can often see clusters by segment—so PCA gave us a 2D “summary” that still keeps a lot of the structure. Part 7: Summary

  • Dimensionality reduction = fewer columns while keeping most of the information.
  • PCA finds “principal components”—the directions of maximum variance—and projects your data onto them.
  • Use PCA for visualization (2D/3D), speed, and noise reduction; avoid it when you need to interpret original features.
  • Always scale your data before PCA. In Python: StandardScaler → PCA → fit_transform.
  • Check explained_variance_ratio_ to see how much each component captures; use n_components=0.95 to keep 95% variance automatically.
